# GLYPH — Phase 1: Game Vision Validation

*Pre-production, Phase 1 of a staged discovery process. Scope is strictly **game vision**. This document deliberately does **not** discuss progression, economy, UI, monetization, or implementation. The only question: **does GLYPH deserve to exist?***

---

## 1. What emotional experience should players have?

The defining emotion is **"I'm a genius."** Precisely: the *delighted disbelief* of triggering a chain reaction you engineered and watching it exceed your own expectations.

That emotion is a fusion of three of the listed experiences, in a specific order:
- **Discovery** (the entry drug) — "I wonder what happens if I put these together?"
- **Power fantasy** (the payoff) — "...oh my god, it ALL went off."
- **Mastery** (the long tail) — "I *meant* for that to happen, and next time I'll do it on purpose, bigger."

Relaxation and competition are *flavors* the game can offer later, but the core emotional contract is **engineered surprise → vindication**.

## 2. What is the primary source of fun? (one only)

**The discovery and engineering of emergent chain-reaction synergies** — the "combo aha." Everything else (spectacle, progression, difficulty) exists to serve that one source. The player's fun is *arranging a system and being rewarded with an outcome bigger than the sum of their inputs.*

## 3. What makes GLYPH different from the genre leaders?

The unifying difference: **GLYPH is the only one where the player deliberately architects the *topology* of a chain reaction and then triggers it to watch it propagate across a space.** Placement is a decision, and the payoff physically *travels*.

- **vs Balatro** — Balatro scores a **linear hand**; the question is "what's my multiplier." GLYPH is **spatial and propagative** — the reaction spreads across a 2D board, so *where* things sit is the whole game. You watch it move, not just tally.
- **vs Luck Be A Landlord** — LBL is a **slot machine**: symbols land where RNG puts them; you cannot choose their positions. GLYPH hands the player the brush — **you choose the layout**, so the cascade's shape is *authored*, not rolled. Architect, not gambler.
- **vs Vampire Survivors** — VS is **real-time reflex/dodging**. GLYPH is **calm, turn-based, deliberate** — a thinking game, not a twitch game. The chaos is on the board, never on the clock.
- **vs Backpack Battles** — Backpack places items that **fight as stats** against an opponent. GLYPH places triggers that **chain as a reaction**; the payoff is a propagating cascade you watch resolve, not a stat duel.
- **vs Loop Hero** — Loop Hero is **passive accretion** over time (place tiles, watch auto-combat loop). GLYPH is a **deliberate one-shot detonation** — you wire a single spark and it all goes off at once.

No competitor combines *deliberate spatial placement* with a *propagating cascade*. That intersection is GLYPH's white space.

## 4. What is the game's strongest hook? (one sentence)

**"You arrange a board of elemental runes, light a single spark, and watch it chain-react into a screen-filling cascade you engineered but couldn't fully predict."**

## 5. What would make someone install it?

Two things, working together:
1. **A six-second clip of a board detonating in a chain** — the most universally shareable kind of moment.
2. **Instant comprehension** — within that clip, a stranger *gets it* ("fire hits the oil, the oil arcs the lightning... I understand this") and immediately thinks *"I could build a bigger one."* Comprehension + the itch to one-up it is the install trigger.

## 6. What would make someone keep playing after 20 hours?

**The combo space is still opening up.** At 20 hours the player has stopped stumbling into chains and started *intending* them, but hasn't seen the ceiling — new elements and interactions keep producing cascades they hadn't imagined. The fun has shifted from accidental discovery to **deliberate experimentation**: "I have a theory; let me test it."

## 7. What would make someone keep playing after 100 hours?

This is the honest make-or-break, and it must be true: **the system has a real skill ceiling and genuine strategic diversity.** At 100 hours the emotion has matured from Discovery to **Mastery + Expression**:
- High-level play looks *nothing* like beginner play (a deep ceiling to climb).
- Many distinct, viable archetypes exist — different "languages" of the system to master, so there's always another way to play.
- Fresh constraints keep recombining the known pieces into new problems.

If GLYPH only sustains Discovery, it's a 20-hour game. To be a 100-hour game, **Mastery must become the engine** once Discovery is exhausted. Phase 1's job is to assert this is *possible* given the concept; it is — chain-reaction systems with authored topology have enormous strategic depth — but it is a hypothesis later phases must defend.

## 8. What is the biggest risk to the concept?

**The predictability sweet spot.** The entire fantasy lives on a razor's edge:
- Too **predictable** → there's one optimal arrangement, the puzzle is "solved," every run repeats, and it's boring.
- Too **chaotic** → the player can't intentionally engineer outcomes, the "I'm a genius" emotion dies, and it degrades into "random pretty numbers" you merely watch.

The cascade must be *predictable enough that you feel smart, surprising enough that you feel delight.* Hitting and holding that band across hundreds of hours of content is the single hardest thing GLYPH must do.

## 9. What is the strongest argument AGAINST building GLYPH?

**It's entering the most crowded indie genre on earth at its most crowded moment.** Post-Balatro, the engine-builder/roguelike-deckbuilder space is flooded with capable clones. The strongest case against is that "spatial chain-reaction" might **not be a big enough differentiator to break through the noise** — that GLYPH becomes a competent "Balatro-like #47," respected by a few hundred genre diehards and invisible to everyone else. Differentiation that's real *to a designer* is worthless if it isn't legible *in a clip to a stranger*.

## 10. What would have to be true for GLYPH to become a breakout success?

- The cascade **feels incredible to watch** *and* is demonstrably the product of **player skill**, not RNG (the "I engineered that" is real).
- It lives in the **predictability sweet spot** (see #8).
- **Elemental interactions are deep enough** that mastery is a long, visible climb.
- It **manufactures shareable moments by default** — the broken-build clip happens naturally, without the player trying.
- It has **one ownable identity** communicable in a single clip — not "Balatro but ___," but a thing people name on its own terms.

## 11. What would have to be true for GLYPH to fail?

- **Placement stops mattering** (RNG layout or over-simplicity makes position irrelevant) → it collapses into an LBL/Balatro reskin.
- **The cascade is passive** — you watch, you don't decide → shallow spectacle.
- **Combos converge** to one dominant solution → solved, no replay.
- **It's indistinguishable from the crowd** in a six-second clip → no install driver, no word of mouth.

## 12. The five design pillars

**Pillar #1 — "One Spark, Total Chaos."**
The entire board must be detonatable from a single deliberate trigger. Every piece a player places should be in service of that one cascade. If a decision doesn't feed the chain, it shouldn't exist.

**Pillar #2 — "You Are the Architect, Not the Gambler."**
Placement is always a meaningful player decision. The shape of the cascade is *authored*, never rolled. The player must always be able to point at the board and say "I built that."

**Pillar #3 — "Predict Enough to Feel Smart; Be Surprised Enough to Feel Delight."**
The sacred sweet spot. The player should be able to *forecast the gist* of a cascade and be *thrilled by the specifics*. We protect this band above all else.

**Pillar #4 — "Elements Explain Themselves."**
Every interaction must be intuitable from common-sense/elemental logic — fire burns oil, water conducts lightning. No combo should require a wiki. Legibility is a feature, not a compromise.

**Pillar #5 — "Every Cascade Is a Story You Can Read."**
The resolution must be legible enough that the player — and a stranger watching a clip — understands *why* it happened. Readability is what makes mastery learnable and moments shareable.

## 13. The five things that should NEVER be added

1. **Real-time pressure / twitch timing / placement clocks.** GLYPH is the calm architect's game; the chaos belongs on the board, never on the clock.
2. **Unintuitive, wiki-dependent interactions.** Anything that violates "Elements Explain Themselves" poisons the core fantasy.
3. **Pure-RNG board layouts that strip placement agency.** The moment the player can't choose where things go, they become a gambler, not an architect — the concept dies.
4. **Any path to winning that bypasses engineering the cascade.** If you can succeed without building a chain, the central source of fun has been made optional, which is the same as deleting it.
5. **Run-interrupting story gates or anything that breaks the cast → watch → "aha" rhythm.** The dopamine loop is sacred; nothing should stand between the spark and the payoff.

## 14. What would 10k / 100k / 1M players say they love most?

- **At 10,000 (the enthusiast core):** "The *depth*." They love theorycrafting, discovering interactions nobody talks about, and the skill expression of building a chain on purpose. They love the **system**.
- **At 100,000 (genre fans):** "It scratches the *one-more-run* itch but feels genuinely fresh because I'm *architecting a board*, not just stacking cards." They love **build variety + the payoff snowball**.
- **At 1,000,000 (mainstream):** "It's *so satisfying* to watch, and somehow I'm good at it." They love the **feel + the legibility** — easy to understand, surprisingly deep, and that universal **"I'm a genius"** moment. The emotion travels even to people who don't normally play strategy games.

## 15. If players leave negative reviews, what would they complain about?

- **"It gets repetitive — I found the best combo and now every run feels the same."** (Convergence / solved-puzzle — the failure of Pillar #1's variety.)
- **"Too much luck — I couldn't execute my plan."** (Placement agency undercut by RNG — failure of Pillar #2.)
- **"It's just Balatro/LBL with a new coat of paint."** (Differentiation didn't read.)
- **"The cascade is pretty but the decisions feel shallow — I'm mostly watching."** (Spectacle outran agency.)
- **"Runs are decided too early / the difficulty feels unfair."** (Snowball balance — an execution complaint, not a vision flaw.)

---

## FINAL VERDICT: 🟢 GREEN LIGHT

**The vision is sound, distinct, and emotionally clear. GLYPH deserves to exist. Proceed to Phase 2.**

Why it earns the green light:
- **A clear, ownable core emotion** ("I'm a genius" — engineered surprise → vindication) that almost no game in the space delivers as its *primary* promise.
- **A single, focused source of fun** (emergent chain-reaction discovery) that everything can serve.
- **Genuine white space:** it is the only concept in its peer group that fuses *deliberate spatial placement* with a *propagating cascade*. The differentiation is real, not cosmetic.
- **A one-sentence hook that is also a six-second clip** — the install driver and the differentiator are the same thing.
- **A credible path through 20h (discovery) into 100h (mastery)** — the long-tail engine exists in the concept.

Why it is green and **not** unconditional:
The two things that could still kill GLYPH are **not vision flaws — they are execution hypotheses**, and they become the explicit charter of later phases:
1. **The predictability sweet spot (#8)** must be findable and holdable. *This is the single most important thing to validate before any commitment.*
2. **Differentiation must be legible in a clip (#9)** — real to a stranger in seconds, not just to a designer on paper.

Because both risks live downstream of vision (they are about *whether we can execute the vision*, not *whether the vision is worth executing*), Phase 1 passes. The vision is worth executing. **Green light to Phase 2.**

*(No progression, economy, UI, monetization, or implementation was decided in this phase — by design.)*
